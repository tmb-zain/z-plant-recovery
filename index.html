+ Why it's built this way

Don't load a reactive fascia

Loading a painful plantar fascia with more pogos is how mild PF becomes chronic plantar fasciopathy — a 6–12 month problem. The Rathleff protocol loads it and trains elastic recoil, so you're not detraining the bounce you want on the pitch. That's why it's the anchor, not pogos.

Ankle dorsiflexion is upstream

Limited ankle dorsiflexion (your right ankle's scar/capsule restriction) forces the fascia to absorb more on push-off. Local foot work alone won't hold if the ankle can't access range — keep the banded posterior talar glide and knee-to-wall tracking running alongside this.
Red team · left side

Check whether it's the left foot

Your left-dominant L4-L5 + Castellvi 2A setup creates asymmetric pelvic loading that often surfaces as one-sided lower-leg overuse. If the PF is left-sided, part of the fix is upstream — glute med and hip stability — not just local foot work. Treating it as purely local would miss the driver.

+ Why it's built this way

Dorsiflexion is the real target

Limited ankle dorsiflexion is one of the biggest mechanical drivers of plantar fasciitis. If the right ankle can't reach full range on push-off, the fascia loads harder to compensate — so this tab sits upstream of the foot work on the Plantar tab.

"Scar tissue" isn't what breaks up

Actual scar collagen doesn't break up with rolling or massage. What improves: joint-capsule mobility (posterior talar glide), tissue glide between layers, neural desensitization, and range tolerance through repeated loading. The talar glide is the highest-leverage item here — keep it daily.
Gate logic

4+ inches, symmetrical

Opens when both sides hit 4+ inches with ≤0.5 in difference. That's the range threshold where the ankle stops forcing compensation up the chain. Hitting it on the restricted right side is the real unlock — don't progress on the left's numbers alone.

+ Why it's built this way

Glute med is upstream of the foot

A weak glute med lets the pelvis drop on the opposite side every step, which sends abnormal load down the chain — knee, shin, and fascia. Strengthening it is often what finally stops a recurring one-sided lower-limb problem that local foot work can't fix alone.
Red team · the real driver

Your left side carries this

The L4-L5 extrusion and Castellvi 2A pseudoarticulation create a left-dominant pelvic loading asymmetry. If your foot or ankle issues are left-sided, the hip is the likely driver — which is exactly why balance and abduction are tested and trained per-side here, not bilaterally averaged.

Spine-safe by design

Everything here is neutral-spine: side-lying, standing, banded. No loaded lumbar flexion or extension-loaded positions. If the hip hike makes your back talk, shrink the range — it should be felt in the side of the hip, not the spine.
